Machu Picchu
img
Cusco Region, Peru
Nestled high in the Andes Mountains, Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Inca citadel that remained hidden from the outside world for centuries. It showcases the Inca civilization’s architectural mastery and deep connection with nature.
Machu Picchu was never discovered by Spanish conquistadors, which helped preserve it from destruction.
Statue of Liberty
A gift from France to the United States in 1886, the Statue of Liberty is a universal symbol of freedom and democracy. It stands at the entrance of New York Harbor, welcoming immigrants and visitors alike.
img
New York, United States
The full name of the statue is "Liberty Enlightening the World.
Chichen Itza
img
Yucatán, Mexico
Chichen Itza was one of the largest and most powerful Maya cities, known for its massive step pyramid, El Castillo. The site reflects the advanced astronomical, architectural, and artistic achievements of the Maya civilization.
During the spring and fall equinoxes, the sun creates a shadow that looks like a serpent slithering down the pyramid steps.
Cartagena de Indias
Cartagena is a walled colonial city on the Caribbean coast, renowned for its colorful buildings, cobblestone streets, and Spanish fortifications. It played a key role in trade and defense during the Spanish Empire.
img
Bolívar Department, Colombia
The walls of Cartagena took nearly 200 years to complete and were built to defend the city from pirates.
Galápagos Islands
img
Pacific Ocean, Ecuador
The Galápagos Islands are famed for their unique wildlife, much of which is found nowhere else on Earth. The islands played a pivotal role in Charles Darwin’s development of the theory of evolution by natural selection.
The Galápagos are home to giant tortoises that can live over 100 years and weigh up to 900 pounds.
